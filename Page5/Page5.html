What is WAN?

WAN stands for Wide Area Network, its exact definition is a very broad term, basically covering everything that is bigger than a LAN. This means that WAN will cover something relatively small:
Connecting 2 different regions together, to something as big as the internet itself, and even bigger, interplanetary networks, could all count as WAN.

The difference between LAN, MAN...etc. and WAN is that WAN is not restricted to a location. This means that you can’t communicate between computers in a WAN through Ethernet cables or wifi, and you can’t differentiate computers by their MAC address. To communicate in a WAN, you have to connect through a TCP/IP suite.

Let us start from a single computer, up to connecting the entire internet.

Take note of the OSI and TCP/IP model on the top right corner of the screen, each technology I mention will have its associated OSI and TCP/IP layer highlighted. Click on it to learn more.

The first step is to transmit data from your computer to a router.

Wired technologies can be used, the most common wired technology is to use ethernet cables. Ethernet is a family of physical and data link layer computer network technology, is widely used in LANs, MANs, and WANs, because of it balance in speed, reliability, and cost. The ethernet technology defines how all data is transferred inside a modern LAN, on the first and second layer of the OSI model.

Ethernet takes the data and places them inside a frame. The frame will contain both the data being sent, and meta-data about where the the data is being sent to via a MAC address of both the sender and receiver, and QoS (Quality of Service information include things like error rate, bit rate, throughput, transmission delay, availability...etc, these info can be used to get the quality of information.) information, and other error-checking / correcting information.

There are many different categories of Ethernet cables, which is explained very well here

Another method is through Wi-Fi

Wireless technologies takes the ethernet standard of transferring frames and packets into a wireless form with the 802.11 standard. There are many iterations of this standard, including the Wi-Fi standards 802.11, 802.11b, 802.11a, 802.11g, 802.11n, 802.11ac, and the most recent wireless technology, 802.11ad. Other branches of 802.11 include regional wireless technologies like 802.11j, which is designed for japan specifically, and other specific use cases.

What most of the 802.11 iterations use is the 2.4ghz radio band. 2.4 ghz is a good balance between range, penetration, and speed. From 802.11ac onwards, the 5.0 ghz radio band is also now supported, an increase in radio frequency means an increase in speed, but at a cost of range and penetration. This means that 5ghz is more suitable in either open office spaces, or small houses, where range or penetration are not needed, and possibly even detrimental to security.

Let's go down the wired route:

The ethernet cable can connect to a switch, hub, repeater,or straight into the router.

Starting with repeaters, repeaters just take in a signal, boost it with an external power source, then send it onwards. This allows for very long ethernet connections to be made, without losing signal or quality, given that there are enough repeaters along the way.

Hubs

Hubs have multiple hardware ports What hubs do is that when a packet comes in from one of the ports (through an ethernet cable). The hub replicates what is sent in out through all the other ports. Hubs are not that common anymore, as they blast the packets they receive to everybody, causing network congestion. Switches (explained below) are much more common nowadays.

There are mainly 2 types of hubs: Passive hubs and active hubs.

Active hubs: Active hubs basically combines the passive hub and a repeater, boosting the signal before sending it to every port. Because it is boosting the signal, an external power source is needed.

Passive hubs: Passive hubs just replicates what is received in one port to every other port. Passive hubs generally don’t need an external power source.

These are different from TCP/UDP ports on OSI level 4

Switches

Switches are smart hubs. They are very similar to hubs, however, they actually have processing power and they read the metadata the packet/frame contains. Switches can figure out which recipient of a particular is for, and only send the packet down that port. As you can imagine, this heavily reduces network congestion. On the topic of network congestion, a factor that influences network congestion is the type of communication system used..

Router

If the packet ever needs to leave the LAN network, it would eventually need to pass through a router. The router is a gateway that provides a connection for the local network to connect to other networks. This creates a MAN.
Most consumer routers combines a switch with the router itself, so that multiple ethernet devices can be connected directly to the router.
If your router is given to you by your ISP also probably combined with a modem so that it can directly connect to your ISP to access the internet.

Miscellaneous

Other devices on a network could include:

NAS: These devices are computers that are specifically designed and optimized to store data, and transfer data over the network. NAS’s allows other devices on the network (and possibly outside the network through the router) to access the data stored on the NAS.
Firewall: A firewall is a list of rules that is checked against all the traffic that passes through it.
These rules can specifies which type of traffic can do what.
Firewalls are normally placed in front of the router that is connected to the external WAN as the first line of defense against attacks, filtering out most of the unwanted traffic. Your personal computer probably comes with a firewall, which has basic configurations like allowing http/s traffic through to your computer so that you can see this website.
DHCP Server assigns devices with their own unique internal IP on the network. Rules can be set to define how the IP's are set out, these rules include:
  • Choosing the range of IP addresses that the router can assign to devices
  • Exclude specific ip addresses from being assigned
  • Configuring the subnet masks
  • Set the lease time for each ip.
Bridges Bridges connect 2 LAN networks together into one network. The most common type of bridge is a transparent bridge, which will seamlessly connect 2 different LAN networks, by combining their router. This means that devices in 2 different networks connected by a bridge can communicate with each other just like they are in the same network.
There are other more complex bridges that can connect more networks together (e.g. multiport bridges). They act like a switch for networks.

Physical Network Topologies

Physical topologies are how network devices are physically connected together (either through wires, or through Wifi technologies), it describes which devices any particular device connects to.

Star Topology

The simplest, and most common physical topology (at least in LAN), is the star topology:

A Central controller (a router or switch in a LAN for example), will connect to all the other devices in the network.
This method has many many positives:
  • Easy to set up (as long as every device knows who the controller is, every device can just connect to it)
  • Cheap (you only need controller)
  • Reliable (There is no complex wiring and connections where bugs and complications can pop up)
  • And easy to diagnose/maintain (If something is not working there is only ever 2/3 places something can go wrong on the network, the sending device, the controller, and the receiving device)
There are disadvantages to this approach however:
  • All the load is placed on the central controller, and since switches can only handle a certain amount of traffic and connections, this method is not very scalable.
  • A lot of traffic on a single point might overload and crash the switch/router.
  • Reliable (There is no complex wiring and connections where bugs and complications can pop up)
  • And if/when the switch breaks, because there is a single point of failure, it breaks the entire network.
  • Each of the connected device only has one connection to the network, which means if the connection breaks, they lose all access to the network

Bus Topology

The bus topology has a central line where every computer connects to, this topology only applies to wired networks:

The advantages of this is:
  • It is easy to add a new device to the network, just attach them onto the end of the line.
  • Requires much less cable length to set up and maintain, since everything is attached to the center line.
  • Can be easily extended with a repeater.
The disadvantages are:
  • Similar to the star mesh, if the central line, or any of the T junctions break, then the entire network is now down.
  • Because all the network devices are connected to one single wire, if 2 different computers want to send data down the bus at the same time, collisions will happen, and the data will be lost. This can be lessened by using a full duplex so that 2 simultaneous communications is possible.
  • Other protocols like tokens can be used to limit who can communicate and when, but then the bandwidth is still significantly reduced.
The bus topology is now rarely used because of these disadvantages

Mesh topology

The final common topology, and also the most common topology for WANs (including the internet), is the mesh topology.

The mesh topology basically means a free for all where everything can connect to everything, creating a “mesh” like net of connections. Because of the many connections that a mesh topology, even if a lot of connections break in a WAN, there almost always still enough connections left that the entire network still functions.
The advantages of this is:
  • Up to infinite redundancy.
  • With the correct routing protocols (which will be explained later), the fastest route can be chosen in the network.
  • No central point that all traffic has to go through
  • No predefined structure that anyone has to follow
The disadvantages are that because everything can connect to everything, things get complicated:
  • Hard to setup
  • Hard to maintain
  • Difficult to add new items to the network
  • No predefined path to follow to get data from A to B

Routing

Since WAN networks are mostly connected with mesh topologies, there are generally no predefined path through the network. This is where routing comes in. Routing defines the route that any network packet will take from one LAN network to another LAN network in the WAN. There are two different ways of setting up routes.

Routing

Static Routing:

  • In static routing, the administrator of the network manually sets routes between 2 different points, specifying which nodes to go to to reach the destination.

Dynamic Routing:

  • This type of routing is what normally happens, where the computer automatically generates best route between different nodes by using specific algorithms set by the administrator.
  • Dynamic routing algorithms mainly use Hop Count and Tick Count to measure the best route to take (to minimize distance).
  • Each routing protocol has to describe how it measures the shortest distance, how it distributes its measurements, and how it deals with common problems like routing loops.

Dynamic Routing Protocols

Routing Information Protocols (RIP):
  • The RIP protocol simply counts the number of hops to reach each destination and chooses the the route with the lowest hop count.
  • This way of measuring speed has the advantages of being easy to calculate, but doesn’t always accurately reflect speed, as different hops can have vastly differing speeds.
  • It deals with routing loops problem by storing how many hops it has currently calculated, and cutting it off when the hop count goes over 15, and storing the distance as infinite.
  • Every 30 seconds, every router that has their routing tables changed sends their entire routing table to everybody else through multicast address (RIPv2).
  • The RIP protocol has now been mostly retired in favour of the newer OSPF protocol.
Open Shortest Path First (OSPF):
  • The OSPF algorithm will only send the changed part of their routing tables, and sends the change to relevant routers (through multicast address) when it takes place.
  • OSPF sets a weighted value for each path depending on the metrics like the time it takes for a packet to travel along that path.
  • Then when counting how long a path is, rather than just counting each hop as one distance unit like RIP, OSPF adds the weighted path value onto its distance.
  • This means that multiple short hops might be chosen over a single long hop.
  • OSPF is currently the most widely used routing protocol.

Ip Addresses

Ip addresses has a similar purpose as MAC addresses, as identifiers of devices connected to the network, however, it is not permanent, nor is it guaranteed to be unique

Each router has a public IP that is visible to the bigger WAN network the router is connected to, and an internal IP, which is what is visible to every component in the internal LAN network that the router is connected to.
For public addresses, the ISP (Internet service provider) will normally provide you with a dynamic IP address. This IP will generally change every time the router is restarted.
For private addresses, the router will normally assign every device their own ip using dhcp. There are some rules/settings that can be set by the administrator. These rules can include:
  • Choosing the range of IP addresses that the router can assign to devices
  • Exclude specific ip addresses from being assigned
  • Configuring the subnet masks
  • Set the lease time for each ip
The private addresses can be set to static by making the device request a specific address from the router.
Private addresses can be used as identifiers within a network, when one computer needs to access data from another computer within the same network, only the private ip needs to be used (sometimes just the MAC address of the individual network device is required).
To send data to devices outside the network, private addresses will have to be converted to public addresses by the router through a process called NAT. When communicating to devices outside the network, NAT makes all the devices within a network appear to have the same public IP (the router's public IP). NAT improves network security by making allowing each computer to not connect directly to the bigger WAN network (usually the internet), but through the router and its firewall.

WAN protocols

To connect to a WAN, most commonly the internet, several WAN protocols has been introduced and improved upon over the years.

WAN protocol graveyard (Not used all that commonly anymore, I am just going to gloss over these):
  • Frame Relay, low cost, super slow (up to 64kbps (that's kilobits, not even bytes)) connection, rarely used now.
  • Point to Point Protocol (PPP), used mainly for dial-up internet, though newer variation has been developed like PPPoE.
  • X.25, this protocol governs the Physical, Data link, and Network layers in the OSI model. While still used somewhat in some countries (e.g. France), it has now been mainly replaced by IP protocols for the network layer, and Ethernet for its data link and physical layers.
  • ATM (Asynchronous Transfer Mode): Seems to be mostly superseded by the IP protocol, out of time to research this one properly, so here’s the link to the wikipedia page:
The most common WAN protocol used now is the broadband protocol, and the standard broadband protocol is DSL , which stands for digital subscriber line. This technology is popular because it allows the use of pre existing telephone lines to transfer data (Caution: DSL is not dial-up, it is much faster and allows for telephone ).
The most popular DSL protocol is ADSL, which is, until recently, one of the best ways to connect to the internet for home users. The A in ADSL stands for asymmetric, and it is there because the ADSL standard does not give the same speed and bandwidth for upload and download. Most users will be downloading much more than they will be uploading. Therefore, it seems logical to take some bandwidth from upload speeds, giving them to the download speeds.
Another DSL protocol is SDSL: Which just stands for symmetric DSL, and as the name would suggest, it just means that upload bandwidth and download bandwidth are the same. This is less useful for home users, but for specific cases (e.g. servers) that could use the extra upload speed, this would increase performance.
The third DSL protocol is VDSL, standing for Very-high-bit-rate DSL, there are many versions of this protocol: VDSL, VDSL2, VDSL2-VPlus: Each version increases speed. However, while this is an improvement on the ADSL protocol, before this standard became widespread, the next standard in this list came along: Optical Fibre.
SONET (Synchronous Optical Networking): This technology uses optical fibre to transfer a lot of data simultaneously over a long distance, typically between ISPs.
After some modification, fibre optic technology are now used to deliver data from the internet on “last mile” from your ISP to your house. It can now connect directly to your house through FTTH (Fibre-to-the-home), giving almost full fibre optic speed. Other “Fibre-to-the-X” include FTT Block, FTT curb, and FTT Node. TCP / IP

TCP / IP stands for Transmission control protocol / Internet protocol. This protocol is made to organise data in a way so that it can be transferred over the internet to the correct location without too much hassle.

Data is arranged in a packet/frame system (see diagram on the right). Each frame contains a header and the content/payload. The header includes information like the destination IP address and other metadata (e.g. source IP address, TTL (Time To Live), version used...etc. ). This packet is then passed to its destination through routing protocols discussed above.
There are two different IP versions currently, the “old” IPv4, which uses a 32 bit address space to store IP’s, resulting in a bit over 4 billion unique IP’s. This is nowhere near enough for each device to have it’s own unique IP. Solutions like NAT (explained above) was used so that an entire LAN will only take up 1 address. Dynamic addressing is also used so that addresses can be taken back when no longer needed.
Then there is the “new” IPv6 protocol, which was formalized all the way back in 1998. This “new” protocol uses 128 bit addresses, which means there is enough addresses for every star in the observable universe to get 100 trillion addresses EACH. This new addressing system will solve a lot of problems involving not having enough IP addresses.
While people have been trying to push the new IPv6 protocol for almost 20 years now, we are still using the old system. Because humans.
IP protocol is a connectionless protocol. This means that there is no need to set up a personal route/connection between you and the destination before you can transmit data. Each packet contains the information that it needs to get to its destination. This results in less extraneous data and infrastructure to be setup before communication can begin.
However, this means that IP is not an 100% reliable method of transmitting data. Errors can occur e.g. Packet loss, the data in the payload can be corrupted, packets can get out of order (since its connectionless, every packet is managed independently).
This is where TCP comes in. TCP checks each packet with its checksum, reorders the arriving packets into order, and sends a request for the packets it lost or got corrupted. This way TCP makes the transmission reliable.

LAN --> Local Area Network
A LAN consists of a group of network capable devices that connects together in a relatively small area. Most LANs usually connect to a router that manages the communication between the localized LAN network, and the bigger WAN network the LAN is connected to. Wi-fi, and Ethernet is usually used to connect devices together in a LAN.
Computers, laptops, phones, "Smart" devices...etc
Houses, schools, laboratories, university campuses or office buildings...etc.
MAC --> Media Access Control
MAC addresses are an unique identifier on every network capable product, specifically on the network interface card. It is made up of 6 pairs of hexadecimal digits (6 bytes), which is a total of 48 bits. E.g. 5B:4D:77:19:F0:BA, this allows for 2^48 combinations ( < 281 trillion). Manufacturers of network cards also try to make sure that every product they produce has a unique MAC address.
OSI --> Open System Interconnection
OSI Model is just a list of "layers" that groups networking protocols into 7 categories.
  • Physical Layer:
    • The hardware layer.
    • Objects that manipulate base electronic signals
    • Things like hubs and repeaters goes here
    • Protocols can include Ethernet
  • Data Link Layer
    • Converts the electric signals in the hardware layers to computer bits that can be processed by the network layer
    • Some error correction happens here
    • MAC addresses are part of the Data Link layer
    • Switches work at this layer
  • Network Layer
    • Routing and routing protocols work here
    • Routers work at this layer
    • TCP/IP and other network protocols work at this layer
  • Transport Layer
    • TCP and UDP protocols work at this layer
    • End to end error checking and correction
  • Session Layer
    • Sets up and maintains communications of applications
    • As the name implies, creates sessions for applications to use
  • Presentation Layer
    • Sorts and organized the data to display it onto your screen
    • Encryptions and decryptions of protocols like HTTPS happens here
    • Makes text appear as text, makes images appear as images, and videos as videos...etc.
  • Application Layer
    • What you interact with
    • The software services like email, RSS, FTP, SSH... etc.
UDP --> User Datagram Protocol
UDP is basically TCP/IP without the TCP part. UDP just blasts packets to the receiver without ever checking if any of them actually got there. UDP relies on the plain internet protocol to get most of the packets there in roughly the right order. UDP is mainly used in voice calling, video streaming…etc. where perfect transmission is not usually required, but speed and low latency is what is important.)
Subnet Masks

Subnet masks allow the administrator to arrange ip’s in a way so that there can be multiple smaller LAN networks inside a WAN or MAN. The subnet mask itself is a 32 bit binary number that “masks” the actual ip address, this is done by converting the ip address to binary, and then overlaying the subnet mask over it. The parts covered by 1are the network addresses, and the parts covered by 0’s are the host addresses.

In the example above, the range of hosts is 256 (0 - 255), and the network range is 256 * 256 * 256 = 16777216. One more thing to note is that the first and last host address (0 and 255) are the broadcast address and the multicast address respectively. This means the actual number of usable hosts is only 254 (256 - 2).

Simplex, Half-Duplex, and Full-Duplex
Communication systems can be classified as simplex, half-duplex, and full-duplex. Simplex communications just means one way communications e.g: a tv remote, data is sent from the remote, but the tv never responds with any data.
Duplex communications means 2 way communications, and both sides can send message to the other at the same time.
Half duplex only allows for communications in one direction at once. The receiver must wait for a “over” message before sending out their own message. 802.11 Wifi is an example of half duplex communication.
Routing Loops
Routing loops are infinite loops created when one of the routes goes down.
Look at the example on the below:

Normally when A needs to go to C, it will use the route through B because 2 (distance from A - B) + 3 (distance from B - C) is smaller than 14 (Distance directly from A - C (imagine a satellite connection). This works perfectly fine until the connection between B-C breaks. When that connection breaks, A wouldn’t know about it, and still send its packets to B. When B receives it, it knows that the connection between itself and C is broken, functionally making the distance to C directly from B infinite. This means that B will try the other route to C, back through A (since 14 + 2 is smaller than infinity), thus creating a loop.
