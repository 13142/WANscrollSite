What is WAN?

WAN stands for Wide Area Network, its exact definition is a very broad term, basically covering everything that is bigger than a LAN. This means that WAN will cover something relatively small:
Connecting 2 different regions together, to something as big as the internet itself, and even bigger, interplanetary networks, could all count as WAN.

The difference between LAN, MAN...etc. and WAN is that WAN is not restricted to a location. This means that you can’t communicate between computers in a WAN through Ethernet cables or wifi, and you can’t differentiate computers by their MAC address. To communicate in a WAN, you have to connect through a TCP/IP suite.

MAN stands for Metropolitan Area Network
MAN's normally consists of a few LAN's, similar to WAN, but cover a much more localized area, ranging from a school to a city.

Let us start from a single computer, up to connecting the entire internet.

Take note of the OSI model on the top right corner of the screen, each technology I mention will have its associated OSI layer highlighted, this will be explained later.

The first step is to transmit data from your computer to a router.

Wired technologies can be used, the most common wired technology is to use ethernet cables. Ethernet is a family of physical and data link layer computer network technology, is widely used in LANs, MANs, and WANs, because of it balance in speed, reliability, and cost. The ethernet technology defines how all data is transferred inside a modern LAN, on the first and second layer of the OSI model.

Ethernet takes the data and places them inside a frame. The frame will contain both the data being sent, and meta-data about where the the data is being sent to via a MAC address of both the sender and receiver, and QoS (Quality of Service information include things like error rate, bit rate, throughput, transmission delay, availability...etc, these info can be used to get the quality of information.) information, and other error-checking / correcting information.

There are many different categories of Ethernet cables, which is explained very well here

Another method is through Wi-Fi

Wireless technologies takes the ethernet standard of transferring frames and packets into a wireless form with the 802.11 standard. There are many iterations of this standard, including the Wi-Fi standards 802.11, 802.11b, 802.11a, 802.11g, 802.11n, 802.11ac, and the most recent wireless technology, 802.11ad. Other branches of 802.11 include regional wireless technologies like 802.11j, which is designed for japan specifically, and other specific use cases.

What most of the 802.11 iterations use is the 2.4ghz radio band. 2.4 ghz is a good balance between range, penetration, and speed. From 802.11ac onwards, the 5.0 ghz radio band is also now supported, an increase in radio frequency means an increase in speed, but at a cost of range and penetration. This means that 5ghz is more suitable in either open office spaces, or small houses, where range or penetration are not needed, and possibly even detrimental to security.

Let's go down the wired route:

The ethernet cable can connect to a switch, hub, repeater,or straight into the router.

Starting with repeaters, repeaters just take in a signal, boost it with an external power source, then send it onwards. This allows for very long ethernet connections to be made, without losing signal or quality, given that there are enough repeaters along the way.

Hubs

Hubs have multiple hardware ports What hubs do is that when a packet comes in from one of the ports (through an ethernet cable). The hub replicates what is sent in out through all the other ports. Hubs are not that common anymore, as they blast the packets they receive to everybody, causing network congestion. Switches (explained below) are much more common nowadays.

These are different from TCP/UDP ports on OSI level 4
LAN --> Local Area Network
A LAN consists of a group of network capable devices that connects together in a relatively small area. Most LANs usually connect to a router that manages the communication between the localized LAN network, and the bigger WAN network the LAN is connected to. Wi-fi, and Ethernet is usually used to connect devices together in a LAN.
Computers, laptops, phones, "Smart" devices...etc
Houses, schools, laboratories, university campuses or office buildings...etc.
MAC --> Media Access Control
MAC addresses are an unique identifier on every network capable product, specifically on the network interface card. It is made up of 6 pairs of hexadecimal digits (6 bytes), which is a total of 48 bits. E.g. 5B:4D:77:19:F0:BA, this allows for 2^48 combinations ( < 281 trillion). Manufacturers of network cards also try to make sure that every product they produce has a unique MAC address.
